About this Project


As a designer for RBC's Tia, a guided Al-based tech-support tool that can help employees resolve common technical issues, one of the projects I led this term was the redesign of Tia's webpage. Through user testing sessions, I validated that the website lacked engaging and informative content for users. In turn, many users had difficulty understanding how to use Tia and learning about Tia's features. By analyzing the product using design thinking and stages of user testing, I redesigned the website as a home for users to interact with Tia while understanding and adopting Tia's capabilities.

What is Tia?


Tia (they/them) is a guided AI-based tech support tool that helps all RBC employees resolve common technology issues. Tia brings a better end-user experience with lower operational costs for the employee and customer-facing contact centers. They resolve common tech issues before the users reach the help desk, encourage self-service solutions, automate tasks, eliminate frustration and simplify the steps to arrive at the solution.

Problem

The current landing page is unengaging and difficult to understand, impacting how users experience Tia.

Employees are not adopting Tia. Many have difficulty understanding how to use Tia and what it does, which increases the drop-off rate and decreases the retention rate.

Problem Validation

To validate my problem, I started off by looking into our insights dashboard for statistics based on the last few pilot releases.

Approximately 45% of pilot users stop using Tia after the second month of access.


50% of fallback messages1 come from users that are using Tia incorrectly.

90% of users have never read landing page content more than once.

1 Fallback messages are user responses that do not trigger any conversation flows through natural language processing. When Tia is unable to understand the user’s message, they will send them to chat with a live tech support agent instead.

My Vision


My vision for the new landing page is to help employees learn how to use Tia and adopt the features offered. In turn, this would drive more engagement and improve the user experience, to enable employees to self-serve and automate their technology issues. Currently, 20 000 employees have access to Tia through the webpage. However, Tia is on track to be launched to all RBC employees by the end of the fall and will be added to major internal employees' applications by September 2022. This webpage will act as a bridge for employees to adopt and learn more about how to use Tia and what they can help with.

How We Got There

Initial Insights

To continue analyzing the problem and determine all considerations, I decided to conduct 7 user testing sessions with both new and pilot employees based on the current website design. Through these sessions, I hoped to gain key information about what employees in different sectors need and feel when interacting with the page and chatbot.

I started off by leading a user testing exercise with my team to understand the objective, target users, questions, and task overviews.

Target Years

  • All RBC employees of different ages, backgrounds, and levels of comfort with technology

Top 3 Questions

  • Did users find an answer to their questions when visiting the webpage?
  • How can we drive value to our key users to use Tia rather than contact the help desk?
  • How can we drive engagement, awareness, and expectations to help users understand Tia’s features?

Tasks Overview

  • Have the participant share their screen and navigate to the Tia homepage
  • Have the participant check the status of their past ticket
  • Have the participant try to connect to an agent
  • Have the participant restart their task

Testing

I then conducted testing sessions with users across the enterprise. I determined the key takeaways from each section and analyzed these facts to form clear insights.

"I’m too busy to read any email updates about Tia, so I’ve never known Tia is able to help with any of these tasks."

"I don’t have a background in technology, so I don’t know how to use this."

"Everything is too lengthy and confusing. That’s not helpful when trying to find answers and I end up ignoring it."

Final Key Takeaways

  • People did not know there was something below the headline and image at the top of the page
  • Users have an outdated view of Tia (are unaware of any new features or updates since they onboarded onto Tia)
  • The page does not provide enough information about how to use Tia
  • Users did not understand how to use the landing page to find answers to their questions
  • Users are busy, they don’t have the time to read any long, complex copy

Employees are Busy

During the testing sessions, I often noted that employees don’t have the time to read detailed content. Users didn’t have time to read updated emails or articles about Tia. Many never read through the landing page. On top of that, users that are using Tia are frustrated. They have been going through a technology issue and coming to Tia in hopes of solving it. Something that is complex and time-consuming would just add to their frustration.

As I was working on the iterations, I condensed the sections when possible and made sure it was easy to navigate for both old and new employees.

This section helps employees that have never used Tia before learn about their features. Gifs will play on the right to show users how to interact with Tia.

To catch the user’s eyes, the latest section is placed at the top of the page and is updated with new content after every release. This will give them a brief summary of new features and help users find out what Tia can do.

Feedback Section

The old landing page generated very few responses for the feedback form, although the button that leads to the form is at the top of the page. This was because the Jira form was very text heavy and required unnecessary navigation to find the correct form to fill out.

My initial idea was to have the form directly on Tia. Since Tia already has access to the employee’s personal information, I thought that having a place to input text and an email imput for confirmation would be enough since we should already know about the user.

However, the information needs to be populated automatically through Jira to keep our insights and metrics in one place. Three separate forms are required to prevent users from providing feedback outside of these categories.

Links that Lead Elsewhere

As I wanted employees to focus on using Tia when visiting the page, I avoided adding links and buttons near the top. Instead, the top section gives a brief introduction to Tia and the main features. The bottom of the page consists of all the links, with the footer leading to resources and a small section with a call to action to visit the Connect site (Connect is RBC’s internal version of “Facebook”.)

Accessibility

One important thing to consider is how well my design supports social inclusion for people of different disabilities and ages. Using Able, a Figma Plugin to simulate colour contrast and colour blindness, I checked if the text, images, and components passed the guidelines. I then spoke with the developer to see if there were any gaps in my design that might affect the accessibility when using a screen reader.

Uncovering Additional User Insights

Before moving on to implementation, I wanted to run a set of individual user testing to receive feedback on the current design from a mix of employees. I conducted 12 sessions and documented the key findings on Mural. (Special thanks to Valeria Gongaza for helping me take notes during the sessions.)

Key Takeaways

  • Users wanted something that made it easier for them to get to each section
  • New employees and interns were confused about some of the content because they were not familiar with the wording

Overall, employees found the landing page easy to navigate and understand.

To solve the first takeaway, I added a menu navigation at the top of the page. This will help users find what they are looking for effectively and call more attention to the feedback section of the page.

The second takeaway focused on the content and wording. With my team, I simplified the test to make it more understandable for users that had just onboarded into RBC.

After implementing these design changes, I spoke with a member of the brand identity team for approval. I then worked with the team for the page to go into development.

My Reflection


This was the first time I had ever led a project that would be launched to such a large range of employees. This was also the first time I had ever worked at a corporate company. At the start, I started having feelings of self-doubt that I was the right person to lead the project. I felt slightly intimidated to be working at a financial institution and wasn’t sure if I would know how RBC’s employees operate well enough. After receiving advice from internal designers and meeting different employees across the bank when conducting the first stage of user testing, I started to gain more confidence in myself. Instead of comparing how capable I was, I found that any conversation I had became an opportunity for me to listen and learn more. Every employee gave me great insight into both RBC’s culture and their way of thinking. It is normal not to know everything, and I would continue to find out more as I progressed.
